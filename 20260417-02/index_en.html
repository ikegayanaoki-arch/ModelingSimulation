  1. Review of governing equations (heat and fluid flow)

Heat conduction equation (general and reduced forms)

Time-dependent form: rho cp ∂T/∂t = lambda ∇²T (with 1D reduction rho cp ∂T/∂t = lambda ∂²T/∂x²).

Steady-state form (no time dependence): ∇²T = 0 (thus in 1D, ∂²T/∂x² = 0).

Notation: capital T for temperature, t for time; lambda is thermal conductivity; rho cp are density and specific heat.

2D heat conduction

Time-dependent: rho cp ∂T/∂t = lambda (∂²T/∂x² + ∂²T/∂y²).

Steady: ∂²T/∂x² + ∂²T/∂y² = 0; T = T(x,y) even in steady state, so partial derivatives remain.

Continuity and Navier–Stokes (2D)

Continuity: ∂u/∂x + ∂v/∂y = 0.

x-momentum (u-equation): ∂u/∂t + u ∂u/∂x + v ∂u/∂y = −(1/rho) ∂p/∂x + nu (∂²u/∂x² + ∂²u/∂y²).

y-momentum (v-equation): ∂v/∂t + u ∂v/∂x + v ∂v/∂y = −(1/rho) ∂p/∂y + nu (∂²v/∂x² + ∂²v/∂y²).

nu denotes kinematic viscosity; pressure gradients align with direction (x or y).

Tensor (index) notation general forms

Continuity: ∂Ui/∂Xi = 0, with i = 1..3 (summation over repeated indices).

Navier–Stokes: ∂Ui/∂t + Uj ∂Ui/∂Xj = −(1/rho) ∂P/∂Xi + nu ∂²Ui/∂Xj∂Xj.

Internal energy (temperature analog, advection–diffusion form): ∂T/∂t + Uj ∂T/∂Xj = alpha ∂²T/∂Xj∂Xj, where alpha is thermal diffusivity; the structure parallels momentum with the pressure term absent.

Conceptual structure: advection–diffusion

Storage (transient) term, advection term, and diffusion term form a common template across scalar quantities (T, concentration) and vector quantities (velocity). Pressure appears only in momentum.

Pedagogical plan: start with pure diffusion (steady), then add time dependence, then add advection, and finally proceed to full Navier–Stokes.

  1. Numerical simulation roadmap (Python; FVM focus)

Scope and objectives

The goal is to numerically simulate 1D steady-state heat conduction and reproduce observed temperature distributions under different boundary and heat-loss assumptions, while comparing them with exact solutions where available.

Progressive complexity: begin with the simplest steady diffusion case, then include source/sink terms (heat loss), then extend to 2D and Navier–Stokes CFD.

Discretization methodology

Emphasis is placed on the finite volume method (FVM) as the main approach; the distinction from the finite difference method (FDM) is highlighted.

Rationale: convert continuous PDEs/ODEs into discrete algebraic equations suitable for computation; this facilitates the handling of conservation laws and boundary conditions.

  1. Problem formulation for today’s 1D steady heat conduction

Governing equation with heat loss term

Starting PDE: rho cp ∂T/∂t = lambda ∂²T/∂x² − Q, where Q represents heat loss (Q > 0 for loss).

Steady-state reduction: ∂²T/∂x² = Q/lambda (after dropping the transient term and rearranging).

Boundary conditions (fixed-end temperatures)

At x=0: T = TL (ambient side; example 25°C).

At x=L: T = TH (heated side; example 1,000°C).

Example length: L = 20 m (motivated by the NHK setup).

Three heat loss cases to compare

Case 1: Adiabatic (perfect insulation)

Physical assumption: no heat transfer from the surface (for example, insulated with a material like Styrofoam); Q = 0.

Governing ODE: ∂²T/∂x² = 0 → linear temperature profile T(x) = Ax + B.

Implication: the linear distribution contradicts the observed convex profile in the experiment.

Case 2: Constant, position-independent convection-like loss

Simplified assumption: heat loss is treated as a constant proportional to a global temperature difference (for example, “highest temperature − lowest temperature” across the bar), abstracting convection with a constant coefficient H; Q is taken as a constant independent of x.

Governing ODE: ∂²T/∂x² = Q/lambda with Q = constant → quadratic temperature profile; this reflects a uniform sink across the domain.

Motivation: capture nonlinearity without position dependence while keeping the mathematics simple.

Case 3: Position-dependent convection loss (more realistic)

Physical model: local convective heat loss is proportional to the local surface-to-ambient temperature difference: q_conv(x) = h [T(x) − TL], with h as the heat transfer coefficient (units J·m⁻²·s⁻¹ per K; representing transfer efficiency depending on fluid, flow, and geometry).

Governing ODE: ∂²T/∂x² = (h/lambda) [T(x) − TL] → a linear ODE with T on the right-hand side; this yields exponential-like solutions consistent with observed convex profiles and stronger losses near the hotter end.

Notes: h can vary with the fluid (air vs. water) and flow conditions (forced vs. natural convection), but it is assumed constant here for tractability.

  1. Analytical solvability and classroom exercise

Exact solutions and their role

The steady 1D cases (1–3) are analytically solvable ODEs with the specified boundary conditions, and they provide benchmarks for numerical methods.

Case 1: linear solution from ∂²T/∂x² = 0.

Case 2: quadratic solution from a constant right-hand side.

Case 3: second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients; the solution involves exponentials shifted by TL, reflecting decay from the hot end toward the ambient temperature.

Linear solution (Case 1)

  1. Basic concepts of FDM and FVM and their differences

FDM

Derivatives are approximated by differences on the grid, based on Taylor-series expansion (for example, central differences). It is simple on a uniform grid. For complex geometries, coordinate transformation is required.

FVM

Common in commercial software. Equations are integrated over control volumes, and volume integrals are reduced to boundary integrals by Gauss’s divergence theorem. It is flexible for nonuniform and complex grids.

Consistency on the same mesh

On a uniform mesh with equal spacing, the final forms of FDM and FVM can coincide, but in general their derivations and treatments differ.

  1. Standard process of numerical simulation (4 steps)

Step 1: Define control volumes, grids, and nodes

Divide the one-dimensional bar [0, L] into n parts. Nodes are indexed by i (interior 1..n, boundaries 0 and n+1). Temperature is denoted by Ti. Boundaries are T0 and Tn+1. Grid width Δxi can in general vary (in the simplest case, Δx=L/n).

Step 2: Discretize the equation

FDM review: d^2t/dx^2 ≈ (Ti+1−2Ti+Ti−1)/(Δx)^2. In FVM, conservation equations are integrated over control volumes and evaluated by fluxes at interfaces.

Step 3: Introduce an iterative method

Solve the implicit coupled system by iterative updates. A flowchart is used to organize the procedure.

Step 4: Flowchart and coding

Implement based on the design. The same procedure will be repeated in later lectures with different targets.

  1. Application of Gauss’s divergence theorem and gradient evaluation on nonuniform grids (FVM)

Reduction of the second derivative

By integrating over a control volume, the second derivative of T is replaced by the difference between first derivatives (gradients) at the east and west boundaries.

Gradient at the east side E

(Ti+1−Ti) / [0.5·(Δx(i+1)+Δx(i))]. On a uniform grid, this becomes (…)/Δx.

Gradient at the west side W

(Ti−Ti−1) / [0.5·(Δx(i)+Δx(i−1))]. On a uniform grid, this becomes (…)/Δx.

Volume integral of the source term

Assuming q/lambda is constant, it is converted to q/lambda · Δx(i).

  1. Coefficient form of the FVM discrete equation and the final expression

Definition of coefficients

CE=1/[0.5·(Δx(i+1)+Δx(i))], CW=1/[0.5·(Δx(i)+Δx(i−1))], CO=CE+CW.

Final update formula

Ti = (1/CO)·[ CE·Ti+1 + CW·Ti−1 − (q/lambda)·Δx(i) ].

Coupled structure and boundaries

The same form gives N equations for i=1..N. T0 and Tn+1 are known boundary values. Because the form is implicit, an iterative solution method is used.

  1. Principle of the iterative method (Jacobi method) and its application to Ti

Fixed-point iteration

Rearrange g(x)=0 into x=f(x), update as xk+1=f(xk), and stop when the convergence criterion is satisfied.

Implementation of Ti updates

Using a predicted value P, update as Ti = (1/CO)[ CE·T(i+1)^P + CW·T(i−1)^P − (q/lambda)·Δx(i) ].

After updating i=1..N, judge convergence using the difference between T and the predicted values. If not converged, update the predicted values and repeat.

  1. Flowchart of implementation and program structure

Preparation

Define arrays such as T[0..N+1], CE[i], CW[i], CO[i], dx[i], etc. For a uniform grid, dx=L/N.

Initial and boundary conditions

Set an initial value such as a uniform temperature. Set boundary values such as T[0]=TL and T[N+1]=TH.

Convergence loop

Jacobi update → compare all nodes → convergence check → continue or terminate. After convergence, output T[1..N].

  1. Notes on mesh generation and notation

Variables and arrays

Interior unknowns are Ti (i=1..n), boundaries are T0 and Tn+1. In the program, the n interior values are treated as the main unknown array.

Generalization of grid width

With nonuniform grids Δxi, regions with steep gradients can be refined and regions with mild changes can be coarsened. The simplest case is Δx=L/n.

Use of schematics

Always sketch the control volumes, node positions, grid widths, and boundaries to avoid confusion.
