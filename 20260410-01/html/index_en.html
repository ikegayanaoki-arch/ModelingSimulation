2. The General Transport Equation
- General form
- ∂φ/∂t + uⱼ(∂φ/∂xⱼ) = D(∂²φ/∂xⱼ²) + F
- Here, t is time, xᵢ is spatial position, D is diffusivity (m²/s), and F is a source or sink term.
- Components of the equation
- Storage: ∂φ/∂t describes temporal change at a fixed point.
- Advection: uⱼ(∂φ/∂xⱼ) describes transport by bulk fluid motion.
- Diffusion: D(∂²φ/∂xⱼ²) describes transport driven by molecular motion from high to low concentration.
- Universality and applications
- The same structure appears in temperature, momentum, and concentration transport.
- For temperature, D is thermal diffusivity α.
- For momentum, D is kinematic viscosity ν, with an added pressure term.
3. Derivation of Budget Equations
- Methodology: control volume analysis
- Governing equations are derived by balancing inflow, outflow, and storage in a small fixed region.
- Continuity equation
- Represents conservation of mass.
- Momentum budget equation
- Based on Newton’s Second Law: rate of change of momentum equals net force.
- Includes storage, advection, pressure force, and viscous stress terms.
- Navier-Stokes equation
- The general momentum equation contains the stress tensor τij.
- For Newtonian fluids, stress is proportional to velocity gradients, yielding the Navier-Stokes equation.
- Internal energy budget equation
- Based on the First Law of Thermodynamics.
- Balances storage, advection, and conduction, with additional work and dissipation terms in the general form.
4. Key Governing Equations
- Three major equations
- Continuity equation: mass conservation.
- Navier-Stokes equation: momentum conservation.
- Temperature/internal energy equation: energy conservation.
- Diffusivity
- Diffusion terms share a common mathematical structure.
- Momentum uses kinematic viscosity ν; heat uses thermal diffusivity α.
- Both have units of m²/s.
